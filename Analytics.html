The Impact of Complete Streets on Sales

Complete Streets Difference-in-Difference Analysis

The decision to adopt a Complete Street policy is a local matter. City and county governments make the decision, often with input from members of the community, and often with incomplete information about how the policy implementation will impact local economic activity. Does a Complete Streets initiative help or hurt the businesses on those streets?

Measuring the Impact

First: Are Businesses on Complete Streets the Same as Others Nearby?

This analysis uses characteristics of local businesses to compare the outcomes for businesses that are located on Complete Streets and those that are on unmodified streets.The first step is to assess whether the two populations of commercial establishments are the same, or sufficiently similar. The sample includes all businesses from the area codes 28801 and 28804, which cover the business districts in downtown and north Asheville, and a majority of the streets with bike lanes. The table below reports basic descriptive statistics for the attributes associated with the two groups.

Table 3 shows businesses type and status as a subsidiary. One important attribute of a business is the type of business that it is.The sample contains 471 distinct Standard Industrial Classification Codes (SIC) codes. The businesses that are most likely to be influenced by the level of foot traffic are kept in their original categories, and other businesses that rely less on exposure to foot traffic are categorized by other.

The attribute in the table is whether or not the business is a subsidiary. As a subsidiary organization a local business has a parent organization that has the potential to help the local site be more resilient in the face of changes in access.Both Tables 3 and 4 show no statistically significant difference between the two groups of businesses – those that face Complete Streets and those that do not.
